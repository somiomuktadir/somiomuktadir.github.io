Advaita Vedanta and the Problem of Knowledge

Advaita Vedanta is one of the six orthodox schools of Hindu philosophy. It was formalized most clearly by Adi Shankara in the eighth century CE, though its roots go back to the Upanishads. The school makes a bold claim: the world as we experience it is not fundamentally real, and the only thing that exists is Brahman, an undifferentiated, formless, infinite consciousness. Everything else, including our sense of being separate individuals, is a product of ignorance.

This is not a claim that most people take seriously at first. It sounds like abstract metaphysics, something that belongs in a philosophy seminar rather than in everyday thinking. But the more you look at the arguments Advaita puts forward, the more you realize they raise genuine problems about how knowledge works, what perception actually tells us, and whether the self is what we think it is.

The Core Claim: Brahman Is All There Is

Advaita Vedanta distinguishes between two levels of reality. The first is paramarthika, which is the absolute or ultimate level. At this level, only Brahman exists. There is no multiplicity, no subject-object distinction, no time, no space. The second is vyavaharika, the empirical or conventional level. This is the world we interact with daily: tables, chairs, other people, our own bodies and minds.

The school does not deny that the empirical world is useful and functional. You still need to eat, go to class, and pay bills. But it insists that the empirical world is ultimately a product of maya, which is not exactly "illusion" in the way we normally use that word. Maya is better understood as a power or force that makes Brahman appear as the diverse world. It is not that the world does not exist at all. It is that the world does not exist the way it appears to.

The analogy Shankara uses is of a rope that is mistaken for a snake in dim light. The snake is not real, but the rope is. When you turn on the light, you see the rope clearly and the snake disappears. Similarly, when you gain correct knowledge, you see Brahman clearly and the multiplicity of the world disappears. The world was never separate from Brahman in the first place.

People often object that this is a bad analogy. Comparing the entire world to a snake on a rope seems to undermine itself. If the world is illusory, then how can we trust any of our experiences, including the experience that leads us to this conclusion? This is the classic self-referential problem that Advaita has to deal with, and it is worth examining carefully.

The Problem of Perception

Every theory of knowledge has to deal with perception. We learn about the world primarily through our senses. You see a tree, you hear a sound, you feel heat. The question is: what is the relationship between your perception and the thing you perceive?

There are broadly two positions. The first is direct realism, which says that when you see a tree, you are perceiving the tree itself, the actual object that exists in the world. The second is representationalism, which says that what you perceive is a mental representation of the tree, not the tree itself. Your brain constructs a model of the tree based on sensory input, and that model is what you are aware of.

Advaita takes a different approach. It says that perception is not about grasping an external object at all. What you are actually aware of in perception is consciousness itself. The tree you see is not something separate from your consciousness. It is a modification of consciousness. This is not the same as saying you hallucinate the tree. It is saying that the tree, as an object of experience, is a content of consciousness, not something that exists independently of it.

This position has surprising parallels with certain strands of modern philosophy of mind. Thomas Nagel, in his famous essay "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?", argues that consciousness is something that cannot be reduced to physical processes. David Chalmers has made similar points about the "hard problem" of consciousness. These philosophers do not arrive at the same conclusions as Advaita, but they share the starting point: consciousness is not easily explained away.

The Advaitin position goes further, though. It says that not only is consciousness fundamental, but the distinction between the subject who perceives and the object that is perceived is itself a construction. In deep sleep, there is no subject-object distinction, yet consciousness persists. This, for Advaita, is evidence that consciousness does not require objects to exist.

The Self: What You Think You Are

Advaita Vedanta makes a distinction between the atman and the self as we normally understand it. The self in everyday experience is a bundle of memories, habits, desires, and identifications. You think of yourself as a particular person with a name, a history, a body, and a set of relationships. Advaita says all of this is part of the empirical self, the self that exists at the vyavaharika level.

The atman, on the other hand, is the pure consciousness that underlies all of this. It is not a soul in the sense of a personal essence that survives death. It is more like the background awareness in which all experience takes place. When you are angry, the anger appears in consciousness. When you are happy, the happiness appears in consciousness. The atman is that consciousness itself, not any particular mental state.

The claim "Tat tvam asi" (You are that) is central to Advaita. It means that your true self is identical with Brahman. This is not a claim about what you should become. It is a claim about what you already are. The work, then, is not to achieve something new but to remove the ignorance that prevents you from seeing what is already the case.

This raises a practical question: if the self is already Brahman, why does anyone need to be told? Why is there ignorance in the first place? Advaita answers that this is anadi, without beginning. Ignorance does not have a cause in the way that physical events have causes. It is simply the starting condition. This is unsatisfying to some people, but it is consistent with the school's broader metaphysical commitments.

The Epistemological Challenge

The biggest challenge to Advaita is epistemological. If the empirical world is ultimately not real, then how do you justify any claim about it, including the claim that it is not real? This is the problem of self-referential inconsistency, and it has been raised by opponents since Shankara's time.

Shankara's response is that the three levels of proof recognized in Indian philosophy (pratyaksha or perception, anumana or inference, and shabda or testimony) are valid at the empirical level. They are tools that work within the system of maya. When you use them to point beyond maya, you are not claiming they can give you direct knowledge of Brahman. You are using them to indicate the direction in which you should look. The actual realization of Brahman is not an intellectual exercise. It is a direct, non-conceptual awareness.

This is similar to how a map works. A map of Dhaka is not Dhaka itself, but it can help you get there. The logical arguments and scriptural texts are like maps. They are not the truth itself, but they can point you toward it. Once you arrive, you do not need the map anymore.

Critics from the Nyaya school, another Indian philosophical tradition, have argued that this is a dodge. If your conclusion cannot be rationally justified, then it is not knowledge. Advaita responds that not all knowledge is propositional. Some knowledge is experiential. You know what sugar tastes like, but you cannot convey that knowledge through propositions alone. The realization of Brahman is like that, except it is about the nature of all experience.

Practical Implications

Advaita is often criticized as being too theoretical. If everything is Brahman, why bother with ethics, social responsibility, or anything practical? This is a misunderstanding. Shankara explicitly states that the pursuit of jnana (knowledge) presupposes a foundation of viveka (discrimination) and vairagya (dispassion). You cannot skip to the conclusion. The ethical and practical groundwork comes first.

Moreover, Advaita does not say that the empirical world does not matter. It says that it matters in a particular way. Suffering comes from treating the empirical self as the ultimate self. When you identify entirely with your body, your career, your social status, you set yourself up for disappointment because all of these things are impermanent. The atman, by contrast, is not subject to change or destruction.

In day-to-day life, this translates to a kind of detachment that is not the same as indifference. You still care about things, but you hold them more lightly. You do your work, but you are not crushed by failure. You enjoy pleasures, but you are not destroyed when they end. This is not a passive disposition. It requires active, ongoing attention to the difference between what is real and what is merely conventional.

Relevance Today

Advaita Vedanta has found a modern audience partly because of its compatibility with certain scientific ideas. The measurement problem in quantum mechanics, for instance, has led some physicists to take consciousness seriously as a fundamental feature of reality. This does not validate Advaita, but it does suggest that the questions Advaita raises are not obsolete.

There is also a practical relevance. The modern world is built on the idea that more is better: more wealth, more status, more experience. Advaita offers a different framework. It suggests that the root of dissatisfaction is not a lack of things but a confusion about what you are. This is not a new observation, but it remains a useful one.

I find the school interesting not because I accept all of its claims, but because it takes the problem of knowledge seriously. It does not assume that perception is reliable, that the self is straightforward, or that the world is what it seems. These are good starting points for any philosophical inquiry. Whether the conclusions Advaita reaches are correct is a separate question, but the quality of the thinking is difficult to deny.

The school also has the advantage of being internally consistent, which is more than most philosophical systems can claim. The distinction between paramarthika and vyavaharika levels of reality is not a trick or a gimmick. It is a serious attempt to reconcile the undeniable reality of everyday experience with the equally undeniable impermanence and constructed nature of much of what we take to be real.

Whether you end up agreeing with Advaita or not, engaging with it will change how you think about knowledge, consciousness, and yourself. That is more than most philosophy can promise.
