The Extractive Equilibrium

Most accounts of Bangladesh's predicament make the same mistake at the same moment: they confuse the smoke for the fire. They observe that students memorise rather than reason, that families herd their brightest children toward the civil service, that the economy clings stubbornly to cheap labour long after its returns have thinned—and from these observations they conclude that the country suffers a cultural affliction, curable by curriculum reform and a generous dose of entrepreneurial evangelism. This is a category error, and it is the most consequential one in the entire literature of Bangladeshi development. Culture is not a first cause. It does not float free of the material order that surrounds it. It is, in overwhelming measure, a manufactured output—the behavioural residue of an institutional architecture that rewards conformity and punishes independent thought because conformity is profitable to those who already hold power. To understand why a nation of extraordinary human density and energy arrives at its 2026 graduation from Least Developed Country status with slowing growth and rising poverty, one must reverse the arrow that conventional analysis draws. The compliant clerk, the rote-learning student, the rent-seeking magnate, the risk-averse parent—these are not the authors of stagnation. They are its characters, written into existence by a script that predates them and reproduces itself through them. Any serious reform must attack the script.

The empirical backdrop is unforgiving, and it is usually misread. Growth slowed to roughly three and a half percent in the most recent fiscal year, the weakest in years and the tail of a steady decline. Inflation climbed near eleven percent before easing only partially, with food prices remaining punishing. The taka has surrendered something close to two-fifths of its value against the dollar across four years. Poverty has risen for three consecutive years atop a stressed banking sector, weak revenue mobilisation, and subdued private investment. These figures are habitually presented as evidence of policy failure. The argument advanced here is precisely the opposite: they are evidence of policy success—success at the only objective the underlying institutions were ever optimised to pursue, which is the preservation of extractive rents. A system does not produce these outcomes by accident across decades. It produces them because every powerful actor within it is responding rationally to incentives the system itself manufactures.

What, then, is an extractive equilibrium? It is not corruption in the colloquial sense of furtive envelopes and the occasional disgraced official. It is a self-reinforcing arrangement in which the rules of the economic and political game are designed to channel income toward a narrow elite while raising barriers against the broad entry of new competitors. Its defining property is stability. It persists not because the right white paper has yet to be written but because it is a coherent system of mutually reinforcing incentives that punishes anyone who deviates from it and rewards everyone who sustains it. In Bangladesh, the extractive architecture rests on several interlocking pillars: bank credit allocated by political proximity rather than commercial merit, which converts the financial system into an instrument of patronage and explains the chronic fragility of the banks; a tax-to-GDP ratio among the lowest on earth, which keeps the state fiscally dependent on the very elites it is nominally meant to discipline; discretionary licensing and customs regimes that turn each regulation into a toll booth manned by someone collecting; and an export model whose profitability rests on suppressed wages rather than rising productivity. The crucial recognition—the one that dissolves the cultural fallacy—is that this architecture is not downstream of bad culture. It is upstream of it. Extractive institutions actively engineer the cultural and educational frameworks that the conventional account mistakes for root causes.

Consider the matter from the vantage point of an entrenched rent-seeker, for incentives become legible only when one inhabits them. What kind of population is most useful to such a person? One that competes for positions within a fixed hierarchy rather than for the creation of new economic value, because new value generates new power centres that threaten the old. One trained to pass standardised examinations and slot into administrative ranks, not to interrogate why the ranks exist. One that perceives the economy as a fixed pie, because a citizenry convinced that wealth is finite will exhaust its political energy fighting over redistribution and patronage—which the elite mediates and controls—rather than demanding the institutional reforms that would let it bake new pies, which the elite cannot control. One whose family structures prize the safe sinecure of state employment, because every brilliant child funnelled into a compliant bureaucratic role is a brilliant child not building a disruptive firm that might one day lobby against an established monopoly. The conformity of the educational system, in other words, is not an accident of colonial inheritance that reform has merely failed to correct. It is a functional requirement of the extractive order. The British designed schools to produce a compliant clerical class because empire needed administrators rather than inventors; the post-colonial elite retained that design because it served the identical purpose beneath a new flag. The continuity is not inertia. It is selection.

This reframing demands that the causal chain be stated with complete explicitness, because everything that follows hangs from it.

Layer Designation Mechanism Observable Expression
First Root Extractive political and economic institutions Politically allocated credit, discretionary regulation, low fiscal capacity, wage-suppressed exports
Second Transmission Engineered culture and education Examination-centric schooling, fixed-pie belief, family career conformity, identity polarisation
Third Symptom Economic stagnation Slow growth, currency depreciation, rising poverty, low R&D, external vulnerability

The reform error of an entire generation has been to assault the third layer with macroeconomic adjustments, or the second with curriculum pilots, while leaving the first untouched. Draining a flooded basement again and again without ever repairing the broken pipe is not a strategy; it is a ritual. Durable transformation must begin at the root, and every intervention aimed at the second or third layer must be judged against a single question: does it weaken the extractive equilibrium, or does it merely decorate it?

Before describing the destination, an intellectual fog must be cleared, for it obscures the moral stakes of the whole enterprise. Money is not wealth. Money is a medium of exchange, an accounting mechanism, a claim. Wealth is the underlying stock of goods, services, and utilities that genuinely improve human life—housing, roads, nutritious food, clinical care, education, reliable communication. In a specialised economy each person produces some narrow form of wealth for others and holds money as a portable claim on the wealth they themselves require. This distinction is political, not merely semantic. An extractive elite is overwhelmingly preoccupied with capturing money—claims on existing wealth—through speculation, arbitrage, and patronage. A productive society is preoccupied with creating wealth—goods and efficiencies that did not previously exist. The first activity is zero-sum, every taka captured being a taka denied another. The second is positive-sum, the total stock expanding.

From this distinction flows the most politically weaponised error in economic life, the fixed-pie fallacy. Children, who produce nothing, naturally perceive wealth as a fixed quantity dispensed by a central authority and to be divided fairly; when this childhood model survives into adulthood it teaches citizens to treat the national economy as a zero-sum game in which any fortune must have been seized from someone else. The conventional account treats this as an innocent cognitive bug. The institutional account recognises it as a useful one, which the extractive order has every incentive to preserve, because a population trapped in fixed-pie thinking directs its energy toward redistributive and patronage claims the elite controls rather than toward the reforms that would unlock the value creation the elite cannot control. The fallacy is load-bearing. It is the cognitive foundation of patronage politics. The empirical truth, by contrast, is that wealth is elastic: a firm that builds software to optimise an agricultural supply chain or extend digital finance impoverishes no one, but synthesises new efficiencies that enlarge the economy's total productive capacity. And here lies the hinge of the entire argument—the capacity to create elastic wealth is precisely what extractive institutions are engineered to suppress, because elastic wealth creates independent power.

Dimension Extractive Equilibrium Positive-Sum Order
Institutional logic Limit entry, capture rents, allocate credit by political proximity Open entry, protect property and contract, allocate capital by merit
Cognitive output Manufactured conformity and fixed-pie belief Cultivated independence and elastic-wealth belief
Primary capital source Land, political arbitrage, suppressed wages Intellectual property, engineering, optimisation
Treatment of labour Inputs to be cheapened Subjects to be empowered
Human-rights alignment Suppresses living wages and free vocation Promotes development, vocation, dignity
Stability mechanism Self-reinforcing rents Self-reinforcing innovation and competition

We arrive now at education, and we must approach it correctly—as the transmission belt by which the root reproduces itself, never as an independent first cause. The examination-centric architecture that marches a child through a relentless sequence of public certificate examinations rewards memorisation, textbook compliance, and passive reception over analysis and creative problem-solving. The conventional reformer says this culture causes stagnation, so reform the curriculum. The institutional analyst replies that this culture is the deliberate product of an order needing compliant administrators and fearing independent minds, that one may reform the curriculum and the order will quietly re-engineer it back, because the demand for conformity originates upstream of the classroom. This is not pessimism; it is the explanation for why successive curriculum reforms in Bangladesh have been diluted, reversed, or captured. The institutional appetite for conformity was never removed, and so it digested each reform in turn.

The population such a system produces can be mapped along two axes—conventional to independent on one, passive to aggressive on the other—and the four regions it yields describe four distinct human types whose political meaning becomes clear only under the institutional lens.

Type Temperament Manifestation in Bangladesh Function within the Extractive Order
Aggressively conventional Rigid rule enforcement, intolerance of dissent Rote-achieving students who police their peers' conformity Enforcers; the human machinery of compliance
Passively conventional Motivated by safety and fear of stigma The memorising majority, dependent on private coaching Ballast; low-leverage administrative labour
Passively independent Quiet, introspective non-conformity Dreamers exploring niche subjects in private Tolerated but marginalised; capable of deep research
Aggressively independent Systematic scepticism, rules provoke testing Rebellious students who challenge authority The existential threat; the cohort the order must neutralise

Read institutionally, the system does not fail to nurture the aggressively independent-minded. It succeeds in suppressing them. Their marginalisation is not a malfunction of the educational machine but its single most important product, because they alone possess the temperament equipped to challenge the order that produced them.

The family, so often blamed, deserves the same charitable rigour. Collectivist structures bind honour and standing to safe, prestigious paths—the civil service, traditional engineering—regardless of a child's aptitude, and the coercion inflicts measurable harm in anxiety, diminished self-esteem, and burnout, compromising the free choice of vocation guaranteed under the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, with a sharp gender dimension that steers young women disproportionately away from enterprise and toward domesticity, capping the nation's creative reserve. Yet parents are not irrational. They respond correctly to the incentives an extractive order presents, because in a system where rents flow through state positions and independent enterprise faces predatory regulation and capricious credit, the civil service genuinely is the rational choice for an individual family weighing its child's security. The family is not the author of conformity but its rational sub-contractor. Alter the incentives and the counsel changes; hector the families while leaving the incentives intact and nothing moves. The same logic governs the fate of obsessive, non-utilitarian curiosity—the deep, self-directed fascination with apparently tedious niche subjects that is the documented seedbed of genuine discovery. An extractive order cannot tolerate it, because such curiosity is the origin of unpredictable, uncontrollable innovation, and so every reform that would protect idiosyncratic inquiry is not merely pedagogical but quietly subversive of the equilibrium, which is exactly why such reforms are so reliably strangled.

Reason itself is the next battleground, and identity is its enemy. When a belief fuses with the self, criticism is experienced as assault and rational debate collapses into partisan defence, which is why disputes over politics and religion so dependably degenerate into emotion. The institutional reading adds a darker layer: extractive orders actively cultivate expanded, polarised identities—communal, factional, partisan—precisely because a population fragmented along identity lines cannot coordinate around the shared material interest of dismantling extraction. Identity polarisation is divide-and-rule conducted by cognitive means; it is not a sociological misfortune but a governance strategy. Against it stands secularism, properly understood not as the suppression of faith but as the preservation of an open, rational public space—the political equivalent of the scientific method, under which a society, like a scientist abandoning a falsified hypothesis, evaluates policy by evidence and human-rights outcomes rather than dogma. A society defends itself against this manipulation by keeping its identities small, treating the label of rationalist as a placeholder rather than a creed, a mere commitment to follow evidence wherever it leads, and by guarding the scarce resource of attention, since the manufactured grievances and ideological wars that consume a talented mind are attention sinks mimicking the complexity of genuine problems while producing nothing—a distraction that is, once again, useful to an order preferring its ablest citizens occupied with anything but its overthrow.

When independent thought finally meets the market it can manifest as a startup, a firm explicitly designed for rapid growth that compresses the output of a forty-year career into a few high-intensity years by leveraging technology against large addressable markets, and the distinction between such a venture and a traditional small business is real. But so is its limit, and it is here that triumphalist development writing commits its central sin, which this essay refuses to repeat. Bangladesh's startup ecosystem drew roughly one billion dollars across more than four hundred and fifty deals across fifteen years, yet the capital is brutally concentrated in a handful of mature firms and more than nine-tenths foreign-sourced, leaving founders hostage to global venture cycles and interest rates, while the surrounding macro environment—subdued investment, fragile banks, weak revenue—has become structural rather than transient. In such conditions a founder must internalise the difference between a company that is default alive, whose existing cash and revenue trajectory will reach profitability before the money runs out, and one that is default dead, whose survival depends on the next round; in liquid markets a founder may remain default dead while subsidising market share, but in capital-starved Dhaka this is recklessness, and unit economics, gross margins, and an early path to profit must dominate over discount wars that incinerate value. Startups take off because founders make them, through the unscalable manual labour of recruiting users by hand, selling directly, and delivering service that does not yet pay for itself—work for which Bangladesh's density and deep mobile penetration are unusually well suited—and investors accordingly weigh founder formidability above market size and execution evidence, prizing the relentless resourcefulness and conversational agility to absorb inconvenient feedback and pivot rather than munging advice to fit a decision already made. The persistent founder, attached to the goal and flexible about the means, is a vessel with a responsive rudder holding its destination while adjusting heading; the obstinate one, wedded to a method against all evidence, is a vessel with a locked rudder, certain to run aground on regulatory and economic reality. Confronting these constraints, the strongest firms have turned upwind and gone global, building SaaS and AI-native platforms for international enterprise and creator markets through a dual-corporate structure that parks the holding entity in Singapore or Delaware for equity and payments while retaining engineering in Dhaka, proving that local talent competes on product quality rather than mere cheapness.

And here honesty becomes obligatory, for this is exactly where the optimistic case collapses. The upwind, globally focused startup is a genuine triumph, but it is an elite triumph. It employs thousands of highly educated engineers and may one day employ tens of thousands. It does nothing whatever for the millions of low-skilled workers whose livelihoods depend on the very low-wage model the country is being urged to transcend. A strategy that enriches the top decile of the cognitive distribution while the bottom half faces displacement is not a development strategy; it is a blueprint for the rising poverty already recorded for three consecutive years. The ready-made garment sector employs on the order of four million workers, overwhelmingly women, anchoring the survival of tens of millions of dependents, and graduation withdraws the trade preferences that have underwritten this model precisely as automation in global apparel threatens the low-wage advantage from the technological flank. No conceivable number of AI-native startups can absorb the displacement of millions of garment workers inside the compressed window between now and the end of the decade. To pretend otherwise is not optimism but innumeracy: a platform employing two hundred engineers does not employ a single displaced seamstress, because the macro-employment crisis and the elite-innovation opportunity occupy entirely different labour markets, and to conflate them is the cardinal analytical error of techno-optimist development thought.

A credible transition therefore requires not one engine but three, sequenced and simultaneous, and the relationship among them is the heart of any honest plan.

Tier Objective Representative Sectors Skill Level Employment Scale Role
First Defend and climb the labour-intensive base Higher-margin apparel, technical textiles, man-made fibres, domestic backward linkages Low to mid Millions The bridge, sustained by productivity not wage suppression
Second Absorb displaced labour into mid-skill work Leather and footwear, electronics assembly, pharmaceuticals, processed agriculture, construction, business-process outsourcing, data annotation, logistics, platform services Mid Millions The decisive staircase between displacement and the digital economy
Third Cultivate the innovation frontier Global SaaS, AI-native platforms, deep-tech ventures High Thousands The apex and aspirational ceiling, generator of foreign exchange

The second tier is the one the startup discourse ignores and the one on which the entire outcome turns, because it alone answers the only question that finally matters—what employs the bottom half—and yet it has failed to materialise at scale for decades. The institutional account explains why: diversification demands new entrants, new credit, new firms, while the extractive equilibrium systematically privileges incumbents, allocates credit by political proximity, and taxes fresh entry through discretionary regulation. Bangladesh remains dangerously mono-dependent on apparel not from cultural conservatism but because its institutional order rewards incumbency and punishes the broad-based firm formation that diversification requires, which returns the argument, inexorably, to the root. The employment crisis cannot be solved downstream because its cause sits upstream.

From this follows a rights-based synthesis that is also, when read carefully, the most hard-headed economics available. A rights-based paradigm treats workers as the central subjects and primary beneficiaries of development rather than inputs to be cheapened, and extends the state's obligations beyond special economic zones and tax holidays into sustained investment in public education, social protection, and fair wages—not as charity but as the mechanism by which a worker acquires the capacity to climb the three-tier staircase and by which depressed domestic demand becomes a growth engine in its own right, loosening the fatal dependence on a single export sector selling into a single concentrated set of foreign markets. The reform that delivers this fails, however, whenever its elements are pursued in the wrong order, and the correct sequence follows the causal model exactly. First the root must be attacked: depoliticise bank credit and clean the banking sector, broaden the tax base away from elite dependence to raise fiscal capacity, replace discretionary licensing with transparent rules, and establish credible independent enforcement of property and contract, without which every other measure is decoration. Only then can the transmission belt be rebuilt, for once the institutional appetite for conformity is weakened, reforms toward critical inquiry, secular rationalism, protected curiosity, and tolerance of dissent will finally adhere rather than being digested back into conformity. And only then does the symptom heal, as diversification, mid-skill employment, living wages, and the innovation frontier emerge not as forced interventions but as the natural output of an order that at last rewards value creation over rent capture. Operationally this means simplifying company registration, reducing trade-license fees, enforcing genuine intellectual-property protection, building local exit pathways through specialised stock-market boards for high-growth firms, and easing profit repatriation to recycle capital—each elite-facing measure paired, indispensably, with mass-facing investment in the second tier and the social safety nets that protect displaced workers while the transition runs its course.

The window is brutally narrow and the temptation correspondingly strong. Growth has fallen and is forecast to recover only partially, inflation lingers near nine percent, per capita gains are thin, poverty has risen three years running, and graduation will soon withdraw the preferences that have masked the structural weakness of the extractive model. The path of least resistance is to treat the symptom: to prop up the failing export model a little longer, to announce one more curriculum pilot, to parade a handful of global startups as proof of transformation while the bottom half of the labour force slides toward immiseration. This is the tyranny of the symptom, and it ends in the middle-income trap sprung by the very low-wage, rent-seeking model that once propelled the country's rise. The alternative is to strike at the root—to dismantle the equilibrium that engineers compliant minds and strangles broad-based value creation, to build the three-tier employment architecture that shields the displaced worker even as it cultivates the disruptive founder, and to align state policy with both the scientific method and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights so that economic progress and human dignity reinforce rather than betray one another. The choice was never between extraction and innovation as techniques. It is between two institutional orders—one that needs its people conformist and poor to preserve its rents, and one that needs them free and productive to generate new wealth. The youth of Bangladesh do not lack the cognitive capacity to build the second. They have been governed by a system engineered to ensure they never attempt it. Changing that system, rather than lecturing the students it produced, is the whole of the task.
