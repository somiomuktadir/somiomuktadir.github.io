1. The basic concept of demography

A population is never still. People are born, people die, people move. Demography is the discipline built to track that motion precisely, and everything else on this page follows from that one idea.

1.1 What demography actually studies

Demography is the statistical and mathematical study of human populations: their size, their composition (age, sex, location), and how both change over time. The word comes from the Greek demos (people) and graphia (description), a description of people, made quantitative. Bangladesh is a clean illustration of why this matters as more than an academic exercise: the population counted was about 71.5 million at the first census in 1974, and 165.2 million at the sixth census in 2022. That is more than a doubling in under fifty years, and no country can plan schools, hospitals, food supply, or job creation without knowing that number, and more importantly, how it got there.

1.2 The three drivers of change

Every population changes through exactly three channels, and only three: fertility (births), mortality (deaths), and migration (people moving in or out). Nothing else moves the count. A rising population simply means births plus in-migrants outpaced deaths plus out-migrants over that period, nothing more mysterious than that. It sounds almost too simple, but it is the entire logical skeleton the field is built on, and every data source in Section 3 exists to measure one or more of these three channels.

1.3 The balancing equation

Formally, the population at a later time is the population at an earlier time, adjusted for the three channels in between:

$$P_1 = P_0 + B - D + I - O$$

where $P_0$ and $P_1$ are population at the start and end of a period, $B$ and $D$ are births and deaths during it, and $I$ and $O$ are in- and out-migration. This is an accounting identity, not a model, it holds by definition. $B - D$ alone is called natural increase, and Bangladesh's own natural increase has been shrinking for decades: the population growth rate fell from 2.84% a year in 1981 to 1.46% in 2011, to 1.22% at the 2022 census. Fertility decline, driven by family planning programs and rising female education, is doing almost all of that work, since net international migration is comparatively small next to the size of the population.

1.4 Population composition and structure

Size alone hides as much as it reveals. Two countries of 165 million people can have completely different futures depending on their age-sex structure, the breakdown of the population by age group and sex, usually drawn as a population pyramid. Bangladesh's 2022 census recorded 81.71 million males against 83.35 million females, meaning women outnumbered men in a national count for the first time in the country's history, about 98 males for every 100 females. A youth-heavy structure, which Bangladesh still has, implies a large cohort about to enter the workforce, a fact central to Section 2's discussion of the demographic dividend.

1.5 Formal demography vs. population studies

Formal demography stays close to the numbers: rates, ratios, life tables, projections, mostly the mathematics of the balancing equation above. Population studies is the wider field asking why those numbers move the way they do, how fertility decline connects to women's education, how out-migration connects to remittance flows and rural income. Your coursework sits mostly in the formal camp, but the two are inseparable in practice: a number like "the growth rate fell to 1.22% by 2022" is formal demography, but explaining that fall through family planning uptake and education is population studies.

Notice that $P_0$, $B$, $D$, $I$, $O$ in the balancing equation are, in practice, estimates, not exact counts, since no country observes every birth, death, and border crossing directly. That single fact is why demography leans so heavily on statistics rather than pure accounting: a census gives a point-in-time count with its own margin of error, a survey gives a sample-based estimate of a rate, and a registration system gives a partial, continuously updated count that has to be corrected for under-coverage. Every technique from your sampling and inference courses, standard errors, bias, estimation, exists in demography for exactly this reason, and Section 3 walks through how.

2. Role and importance of demographic studies

Demographic numbers are not trivia. Nearly every major policy decision a government makes, how many schools to build, how many jobs need creating, how much rice the country needs to grow, starts from a demographic fact.

2.1 Planning around population size and structure

Governments plan against numbers, not vibes. A ministry cannot decide how many primary schools to build without knowing how many children will be of school age five years from now, and that requires knowing today's age structure and applying survival and fertility assumptions to it, exactly the balancing equation from 1.3, projected forward. The 2022 census recorded Dhaka Division alone at 44.4 million people against Barisal Division's 9.1 million, a more than four-fold gap that by itself justifies why infrastructure and health budgets are not divided equally across divisions.

2.2 The demographic dividend

When a country's working-age population grows faster than its dependent population (children and the elderly), it opens a window called the demographic dividend: more workers relative to dependents, in principle more savings, investment, and growth per person, provided jobs exist to absorb them. Bangladesh's steep fertility decline since the 1970s, brought about substantially through national family planning programs, is exactly what opened this window. Whether the dividend is actually captured depends on whether education and employment keep pace, which is why demographic projections feed directly into labour and education policy, not just population policy.

2.3 Demography as an early-warning system

Demographic monitoring also catches reversals early, before they become crises. Bangladesh's total fertility rate fell for decades toward the replacement level of about 2.1 children per woman, but the Multiple Indicator Cluster Survey published in late 2025 by BBS and UNICEF recorded a fertility rate of 2.4, up from a baseline of 2.3, the first measured rise in decades. Officials point to a prolonged shortage of family-planning field staff and disrupted contraceptive supply during a period of government transition as likely drivers. Whether or not that single estimate holds up in later surveys, the example shows exactly what demographic studies are for: a policy problem (weakening family-planning delivery) became visible as a number before it became visible as a crisis.

2.4 Monitoring development goals

Bangladesh's Sustainable Development Goal commitments are tracked substantially through demographic indicators: the crude birth rate, crude death rate, infant and under-five mortality rates, and maternal mortality ratio, all published annually by BBS through the Sample Vital Registration System discussed in Section 3.2. Without a demographic monitoring system producing these numbers every year rather than once a decade, there would be no way to know whether a health program is working before an entire ten-year census cycle has passed.

2.5 Understanding migration and its consequences

Migration, the third arm of the balancing equation, matters enormously for a country like Bangladesh, where millions of workers move abroad and remittances form a major part of household income in many districts. Demographic study of migration, who leaves, from where, and what that does to the age and sex structure left behind, feeds directly into policy on remittance channels, rural labour markets, and even household composition, since out-migration by working-age men can leave a measurably different age-sex structure in the sending districts than in the country as a whole.

Here is the connection back to your own coursework: almost none of the numbers in this section come from counting every single person every single year. A census happens once a decade, so nearly everything in between, growth rates, fertility trends, mortality trends, is an estimate built from a sample or a partial registration system, carrying sampling error and potential bias just like any statistic you compute in an inference course. The MICS fertility estimate in 2.3 is a single sample-based number, not a full enumeration, which is exactly why demographers wait to see whether it repeats in the next survey round before treating it as a real trend rather than sampling noise. Section 3 is about where these estimates actually come from, and what each source can and cannot tell you.

3. Sources of demographic data

Every number in Sections 1 and 2 had to come from somewhere. No single source is complete and continuous at once, so real countries, Bangladesh included, stitch several sources together, each covering the gaps the others leave.

3.1 The census

A census is a complete enumeration: every single person in the country, counted individually, at the same reference point in time, usually once a decade. Bangladesh's national statistical office, the Bangladesh Bureau of Statistics (BBS), has run six censuses since independence: 1974, 1981, 1991, 2001, 2011, and 2022. The 2022 round, Bangladesh's first fully digital census, counted 165,158,616 people, up from 144.0 million in 2011, and recorded, for the first time, more women than men nationally, 83.35 million against 81.71 million.

A census is the only source with the reach to give reliable numbers for the smallest administrative units, down to a single union or ward, which is exactly why it anchors everything else in this section: nearly every survey and every projection between census years leans on the population counts and the maps of enumeration areas that the census produces. Its weakness is the mirror image of its strength: it is expensive and disruptive to run this thoroughly, so it happens only once every ten years, leaving nine years in between where the population keeps moving but nobody has taken a full count.

3.2 Civil registration and the vital registration system

Civil registration is the continuous, permanent recording of vital events, births, deaths, marriages, divorces, as they happen, ideally within days of each event. In principle this is the best possible source, since it needs no sampling and no waiting for the next census, every event is captured the moment it occurs. In practice, Bangladesh's civil registration system has struggled with coverage: national birth registration has hovered around half of all births, and in 2020 only about 4.5% of births and 36% of deaths were registered within the legally required 45-day window.

To fill that gap, BBS runs a separate system called the Sample Vital Registration System (SVRS), in place since 1980. Rather than trying to register every event across the whole country, the SVRS tracks vital events in a fixed sample of areas using two independent recording channels at once, a locally recruited registrar who logs events as they occur, and a separate periodic enumeration. Events caught by both channels, by only one, or by neither, are then reconciled using a statistical method called the Chandrasekaran-Deming technique to estimate the true number of events, including the ones both channels missed. The SVRS is what actually produces Bangladesh's annual crude birth rate, crude death rate, total fertility rate, infant and under-five mortality rates, and maternal mortality ratio, the very indicators referenced in Section 2.4, filling the nine-year silence left by the census in 3.1.

3.3 Sample surveys

A sample survey collects detailed information from a carefully selected subset of the population rather than everyone, trading complete coverage for depth and speed. Because a census only asks a handful of questions of every person, and asking many detailed questions of everyone would be prohibitively slow and costly, detailed topics are instead studied through surveys built on a scientifically drawn sample.

Bangladesh runs several major ones. The Bangladesh Demographic and Health Survey (BDHS), led by the National Institute of Population Research and Training (NIPORT), has run nine rounds since 1993-94, most recently in 2022 with over 30,000 households, covering fertility, child and maternal mortality, and family planning in far more depth than a census ever could. The Multiple Indicator Cluster Survey (MICS), run jointly by BBS and UNICEF, is the source behind the fertility uptick discussed in 2.3. The Household Income and Expenditure Survey (HIES), now in its seventeenth round since 1973-74, and the Labour Force Survey (LFS), both add socioeconomic and employment detail to the demographic picture.

These surveys are not free-floating, they lean directly on the census from 3.1: a survey's sample is typically drawn from the enumeration areas the most recent census mapped out. The 2014 BDHS round, for instance, selected 600 enumeration areas out of a master list of 293,579 areas the census had delineated, using probability-proportional-to-size sampling. Without a recent census providing that list, there would be no reliable frame from which to draw a representative sample at all.

3.4 Population registers

A population register is a continuously maintained administrative list of residents, updated as people are born, die, or move, rather than recounted from scratch. Where a census is a single photograph, a register is closer to a running video. Bangladesh does not run a population register in the strict Scandinavian sense, but the National Identity Registration Wing (NID Wing) functions as a close, working equivalent: originally built by the Election Commission to prepare a biometric database of roughly 81.3 million eligible voters ahead of the 2008 election, it now issues a National Identity Card to every citizen aged 18 and above, embedded with biometric data on smart cards since 2016, and required for banking, mobile registration, passports, and most other formal services.

Because holding an NID has become a practical precondition for participating in formal Bangladeshi life, the database is continuously updated in a way no periodic survey or ten-yearly census can match, though it was built for electoral and identification purposes rather than demographic research, so it lacks much of the socioeconomic detail a census or BDHS would collect.

3.5 Other sources

A country's demographic picture also draws on sources that were not built for demography at all. Administrative records from other sectors, school enrollment rolls, health facility logs, passport and overseas work-permit records from the Bureau of Manpower, Employment and Training, all carry demographic information as a byproduct of their real purpose, and are especially valuable for tracking labour migration in a country where remittances from workers abroad are a major part of the national economy. International bodies such as the UN Population Division and the World Bank compile and harmonize national figures like these into comparable cross-country series, useful for comparison but built from the same underlying national sources rather than collected independently. More recently, anonymized mobile phone location data has begun to be explored, still experimentally, as a way of tracking internal migration and urbanization between censuses.

Two links back to your own courses are worth naming directly. First, a census functions as a sampling frame, the enumeration areas it maps become the primary sampling units that BDHS, MICS, and HIES draw their samples from, so the quality of every survey estimate in Bangladesh is bounded by how current the underlying census still is. Second, the SVRS's dual-record reconciliation in 3.2 is a real-world case of capture-recapture estimation: the same logic used in ecology to estimate an animal population from two independent trapping sessions, applied here to estimate the true number of births and deaths from two independent recording channels, neither of which is complete on its own. Both are ordinary estimation problems from your inference coursework, just wearing a demographic name.
