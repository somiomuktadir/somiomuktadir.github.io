1. Concepts, terminology, and design

A sample is only useful if the path from sample to population is exact. That path starts with a small, fixed vocabulary, and every later formula in this page is built directly on top of it.

1.1 Population, units, and the sampling frame

The population is the complete set of $N$ units a study wants to say something about. A sampling unit is the object actually selected during sampling, a household, a school, a district, and can differ from the element, the object actually measured: a household is a sampling unit, but each person inside it is an element. The sampling frame is the physical list, map, or register from which units are drawn. A frame is rarely identical to the population: it can miss units that exist (undercoverage), include units that no longer exist (overcoverage), or list a unit more than once (duplication), and every one of these frame problems becomes an error in the final estimate no matter how carefully the sample itself is drawn.

1.2 Parameter and statistic

A parameter is a fixed, usually unknown number describing the population: the population mean $\bar{Y} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} Y_i$, the population total $Y = \sum_{i=1}^{N} Y_i$, the population proportion $P$. A statistic is a quantity computed from the sample alone: the sample mean $\bar{y} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i$, the sample proportion $p$. The parameter is a constant; the statistic is a random variable, since a different draw of $n$ units from the same population produces a different value. Everything in Section 3 is about describing the behaviour of that randomness.

1.3 The role of sampling theory

Sampling theory exists to answer one question honestly: how much can be trusted from a partial count. A full census removes the question entirely, since $n = N$ leaves nothing unobserved, but a census is often too slow, too costly, or physically impossible, testing every manufactured unit for strength destroys the stock being tested. Sampling theory supplies the machinery, built on probability, to draw a partial set of units and still make a defensible, quantified statement about the whole population, including exactly how far off that statement is likely to be. That last part, an honest, computable margin of error rather than a guess, is what separates sampling theory from simply looking at "some data" and is the direct reason your coursework treats a statistic as having a sampling distribution rather than a single fixed value.

The comparison also runs the other way: a rushed, poorly supervised census carries every source of non-sampling error discussed in 3.4 at full national scale, while a smaller, carefully executed sample survey can be more accurate overall even though it alone carries sampling error. Completeness is not the same thing as accuracy.

1.4 Requirements of a good sampling design

A sampling design earns trust by satisfying a short list of properties together, not any single one alone.

  • Representativeness. The sample's structure should mirror the population's structure on the characteristics that matter for the study, so conclusions drawn from it generalise.
  • Known, computable error. The design must allow the sampling error of an estimate to be calculated from the sample itself, not merely assumed. As Section 2.2 makes precise, this in practice requires probability sampling.
  • Adequate precision for the cost. A design is judged against a budget, not in isolation. The goal is the smallest error achievable for the resources available, or equivalently the lowest cost for a required error, addressed directly in Section 3.5.
  • Absence of bias. Systematic distortion introduced by the frame, the selection procedure, or nonresponse should be minimised, since no amount of increased sample size corrects a biased procedure.
  • Practical feasibility. The design has to be executable in the field, with available time, staff, and logistics, and simple enough that fieldworkers apply it consistently without deviating from the plan.

Hold onto the frame problem in 1.1 and the practicality requirement in 1.4 together. A mathematically elegant design applied to a bad frame, or executed by an untrained field team, produces an estimate whose formal standard error looks small while its actual error is large. The formulas from Section 3 describe sampling error only; they say nothing about frame or fieldwork problems, which is precisely why 3.4 treats sampling error and non-sampling error as two separate, additive sources rather than one.

2. Selecting and executing a sample

Once the concepts in Section 1 are fixed, a survey still has to be built and run. This section moves from planning to the actual mechanics of drawing units and weighting what comes back.

2.1 Steps in a sample survey

A sample survey moves through a fixed sequence, and skipping or reordering a step tends to reappear later as an error that looks like something else entirely.

  • Define the objective. State exactly what is to be estimated, and for which population and reference period.
  • Define the target population and units. Fix what counts as a unit and an element, per 1.1.
  • Construct or obtain the sampling frame. Assess it for the coverage problems in 1.1 before drawing anything from it.
  • Choose the sampling design. Select among the designs in 2.2, guided by the requirements in 1.4 and the cost structure of the survey.
  • Determine the sample size. Fix $n$ against a target precision and budget, worked through in 3.5 and 3.6.
  • Select the sample. Apply the chosen design mechanically, without substituting units in the field for convenience.
  • Design the instrument and collect data. Build the questionnaire or measurement protocol and carry out fieldwork.
  • Process data and compute estimates. Apply the weights from 2.4 and compute the parameter estimates together with their standard errors.
  • Report results with their margins of error. An estimate without its precision attached is an incomplete answer to the objective stated in step one.

2.2 Probability and non-probability sampling

In probability sampling, every unit in the frame has a known, nonzero probability of selection, fixed in advance by the design. Simple random sampling, systematic sampling, stratified sampling, and cluster or multi-stage sampling all belong to this family. Because the selection probabilities are known, the sampling error of an estimate can be computed directly from the sample, satisfying the second requirement in 1.4.

In non-probability sampling, units are chosen by convenience, judgment, quota, or referral, and their selection probabilities are unknown or undefined. Convenience sampling (whoever is easiest to reach), purposive or judgment sampling (an expert hand-picks units believed to be typical), quota sampling (fill fixed category counts without random selection within them), and snowball sampling (existing respondents refer new ones) are the common forms. These designs can still be useful, cheap, fast, sometimes the only option when no frame exists, but the sampling error of an estimate from them cannot be computed in the way Section 3 describes, and selection bias cannot be ruled out by the design itself. That gap is exactly why 1.4 named computable error as a requirement rather than an option.

2.3 Selection probability and inclusion probability

The selection probability, or draw-by-draw probability, is the probability a specific unit is chosen at one particular draw. Under simple random sampling without replacement (SRSWOR) from a population of size $N$, the first draw gives each unit probability $1/N$; a unit not drawn first has probability $1/(N-1)$ on the second draw, and so on, since the pool shrinks after each draw.

The inclusion probability $\pi_i$ is the probability that unit $i$ ends up in the final sample of size $n$ at all, across every draw combined, and it is this quantity, not the single-draw probability, that estimation formulas actually use. For SRSWOR of size $n$ from $N$, every unit has the same inclusion probability:

$$\pi_i = \frac{n}{N}$$

Designs do not have to give every unit equal inclusion probability. In probability-proportional-to-size (PPS) sampling, a unit's inclusion probability is set proportional to a known size measure, say a district's population or a school's enrolment, so that large, more influential units are more likely to enter the sample: $\pi_i \propto Z_i$ for a size measure $Z_i$. The second-order inclusion probability $\pi_{ij}$, the probability that units $i$ and $j$ are both in the sample, is needed whenever the variance of an estimator has to account for the fact that draws are not independent, which is always true under sampling without replacement.

2.4 Sampling weight

The sampling weight (or design weight) of unit $i$ is the inverse of its inclusion probability:

$$w_i = \frac{1}{\pi_i}$$

and it has a direct reading: $w_i$ is the number of population units that sampled unit $i$ stands in for. Under SRSWOR, $w_i = N/n$ for every unit, all sampled units represent the same number of population units. Under PPS, units with a higher inclusion probability get a smaller weight, correcting for having been oversampled on purpose. Population totals and means are then estimated by weighting each sampled observation before summing, the Horvitz-Thompson estimator:

$$\hat{Y} = \sum_{i \in s} \frac{y_i}{\pi_i} = \sum_{i \in s} w_i\, y_i$$

where $s$ denotes the set of sampled units. In practice, design weights are rarely used exactly as computed, they are usually adjusted further for nonresponse (inflating the weight of respondents to stand in for similar non-respondents) and calibrated so weighted totals for known characteristics, such as age or region, match external, trusted figures, but both adjustments start from the design weight $w_i$ defined here.

2.5 With and without replacement sampling

Under sampling with replacement (WR), a selected unit is returned to the frame before the next draw, so it can be drawn again, and every draw is independent with the same probability $1/N$. Under sampling without replacement (WOR), a selected unit is removed from the frame, so it cannot repeat, and draws are no longer independent, later draws depend on which units are already gone.

The practical difference shows up in the variance of the sample mean. For a population variance $S^2 = \frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2$,

$$V(\bar{y})_{WR} = \frac{S^2}{n} \qquad\qquad V(\bar{y})_{WOR} = \frac{S^2}{n}\left(1 - \frac{n}{N}\right)$$

The extra factor $\left(1 - \frac{n}{N}\right)$ is the finite population correction (fpc). It is always between 0 and 1 for $n < N$, so WOR gives a strictly smaller variance than WR for the same $n$, sampling without replacement is more efficient, because it never wastes a draw re-measuring a unit already known. As $n$ approaches $N$, the fpc approaches zero, correctly signalling that a near-complete sample carries almost no sampling uncertainty at all. When $N$ is very large relative to $n$, the fpc is close to 1 and the WR and WOR variances converge, which is why the correction is often dropped for large populations sampled at a small fraction.

3. Precision, error, and sample size

An estimate is incomplete without a statement of how much it can be trusted. This section defines that trust precisely, then uses it to decide how large a sample needs to be in the first place.

3.1 Bias

The bias of an estimator $\hat{\theta}$ for a parameter $\theta$ is the gap between its average value over all possible samples and the true parameter:

$$B(\hat{\theta}) = E(\hat{\theta}) - \theta$$

An estimator is unbiased when $B(\hat{\theta}) = 0$, meaning it is correct on average across repeated sampling, though any single sample's estimate can still land above or below $\theta$. The sample mean under SRSWOR is unbiased for the population mean, $E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y}$. A common trap sits in the sample variance: dividing by $n$ instead of $n-1$, $\frac{1}{n}\sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2$, systematically underestimates the population variance, which is exactly why the unbiased sample variance uses $n-1$ in the denominator instead.

3.2 Variance and precision

The variance of an estimator measures how much it would swing from one sample to the next:

$$V(\hat{\theta}) = E\big[(\hat{\theta} - E(\hat{\theta}))^2\big]$$

Precision is exactly this: how tightly repeated samples' estimates cluster around their own average. Precision is not the same as being close to the truth, an estimator can be highly precise, tightly clustered, while clustered around the wrong value if it is biased. Section 1.4's demand for adequate precision is a demand about variance specifically, and it is variance, through the standard error $\sqrt{V(\hat\theta)}$, that shrinks as sample size grows, which is the entire basis for Section 3.5.

3.3 Mean square error

The mean square error (MSE) combines both ideas into one number: the average squared distance between the estimator and the true parameter.

$$MSE(\hat{\theta}) = E\big[(\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2\big] = V(\hat{\theta}) + \big[B(\hat{\theta})\big]^2$$

For an unbiased estimator, $MSE = V(\hat{\theta})$, variance and MSE coincide. But a small, deliberate amount of bias can sometimes buy a large reduction in variance, and MSE is the quantity that judges whether that trade is worth it. A ratio estimator, which uses an auxiliary variable strongly correlated with the variable of interest, is a standard example: it carries a small bias in most designs, yet when the correlation is strong its variance can fall so much that its MSE beats that of the unbiased sample mean. Bias alone, and variance alone, both understate what MSE measures completely.

3.4 Errors in a sample survey and a census

Sampling error exists only because a partial set of units stands in for the whole population. It is exactly what $V(\hat\theta)$ in 3.2 quantifies, it shrinks as $n$ grows, and it vanishes entirely in a full census, where $n = N$ and the fpc from 2.5 collapses to zero.

Non-sampling error is everything else, and it affects a census just as much as a sample, often more, since a census has no sampling error to offset against its own execution problems. Its main sources are coverage error (the frame problem from 1.1), nonresponse (units that refuse or cannot be reached), measurement or response error (a question misunderstood, a respondent misremembering, an instrument miscalibrated), and processing error (mistakes in data entry, coding, or editing). Unlike sampling error, non-sampling error does not automatically shrink with a larger $n$; a bigger operation with more enumerators and more forms to process can, if not tightly supervised, accumulate more of it, not less. Total error is best understood as an approximate sum of the two, quantifiable sampling error plus largely unquantifiable non-sampling error:

$$MSE(\hat{\theta}) \approx \underbrace{V(\hat{\theta})}_{\text{sampling error}} + \underbrace{\text{coverage, nonresponse, measurement, processing error}}_{\text{non-sampling error}}$$

which is the direct source of the point raised in 1.3: a well-run sample survey, carrying only the first term, can have lower total error than a census whose second term has grown large through scale alone.

3.5 Sample size determination: the basic case

Fix a desired margin of error $d$ (also called the bound on the error of estimation) and a confidence level, giving a multiplier $z$ (for 95% confidence, $z = 1.96$). For estimating a population mean under SRSWOR with population variance $S^2$, the required sample size ignoring the finite population correction is:

$$n_0 = \frac{z^2 S^2}{d^2}$$

and, since a finite population cannot need more units sampled than it has, the fpc is folded back in as a downward adjustment:

$$n = \frac{n_0}{1 + \dfrac{n_0}{N}}$$

For a population proportion $P$, replace $S^2$ with $P(1-P)$:

$$n_0 = \frac{z^2\, P(1-P)}{d^2}$$

When $P$ is not known in advance, $P = 0.5$ is used, since $P(1-P)$ is maximised at $P=0.5$, giving the most conservative, largest possible required sample size.

Worked example. A survey wants to estimate a proportion to within $d = 0.03$ (three percentage points) at 95% confidence, with no prior estimate of $P$:

$$n_0 = \frac{(1.96)^2 (0.5)(0.5)}{(0.03)^2} = \frac{0.9604}{0.0009} \approx 1068$$

If the population is finite with $N = 5000$, the fpc brings this down to:

$$n = \frac{1068}{1 + \dfrac{1068}{5000}} = \frac{1068}{1.2136} \approx 880$$

A smaller, more concentrated population needs proportionally fewer sampled units for the same precision, exactly what the fpc is built to capture.

3.6 Sample size determination: complex scenarios

Real surveys rarely draw a plain SRS from one undivided population, and sample size formulas extend accordingly.

Stratified allocation. When the population is divided into $H$ strata of sizes $N_1, \dots, N_H$ with standard deviations $S_1, \dots, S_H$, and every unit costs the same to sample, Neyman allocation distributes a fixed total sample size $n$ across strata to minimise overall variance:

$$n_h = n \cdot \frac{N_h S_h}{\sum_{h=1}^{H} N_h S_h}$$

Strata with more units or more internal variability receive a larger share of the sample, since that is where the most uncertainty is concentrated. When per-unit cost $c_h$ differs by stratum, this generalises to optimum allocation, which additionally sends fewer units to expensive-to-survey strata:

$$n_h \propto \frac{N_h S_h}{\sqrt{c_h}}$$

Cluster and multi-stage designs. Sampling intact clusters (villages, schools, city blocks) instead of individuals is cheaper to field but less efficient statistically, since units inside the same cluster tend to resemble each other. This inflation is captured by the design effect:

$$DEFF = \frac{V(\hat{\theta})_{\text{complex design}}}{V(\hat{\theta})_{\text{SRS, same } n}} \approx 1 + (\bar{m}-1)\rho$$

where $\bar{m}$ is the average cluster size and $\rho$ is the intraclass correlation, how similar units within a cluster are to each other. A basic SRS-based sample size $n_{SRS}$ from 3.5 must then be inflated to $n_{complex} = n_{SRS} \times DEFF$ to reach the same precision under the actual design; equivalently, the effective sample size actually achieved is only $n_{eff} = n_{complex} / DEFF$.

Nonresponse. If a survey expects a response rate $r$ (for example $r = 0.8$), the fielded sample size must be inflated so that the number of completed responses still meets the target: $n' = n / r$.

Multiple domains. When a survey must produce reliable estimates not just nationally but for several sub-groups or regions separately, sample size is driven by whichever domain needs the largest $n$ for its own required precision, then summed across domains, not by the precision of the national total alone.

Every complex-scenario adjustment above does the same conceptual thing: it starts from the basic $n_0$ in 3.5 and multiplies or divides by a correction factor, $DEFF$ for clustering, $1/r$ for nonresponse, a stratum share for allocation, that accounts for one specific way real fieldwork departs from the clean SRSWOR assumptions used to derive $n_0$ in the first place. None of these factors change what precision, bias, or MSE mean; they change how large $n$ has to be to actually deliver the precision Section 1.4 demanded, under conditions a textbook formula alone does not see.
