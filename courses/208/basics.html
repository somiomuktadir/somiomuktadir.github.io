1. Logic and proofs

Logic is the machinery of reasoning correctly. Before proving anything, you need a stable vocabulary for what counts as true, what follows from what, and what makes an argument valid rather than merely persuasive.

1.1 Statements and truth values

A statement (or proposition) is a declarative sentence that is definitively true or false, never both and never neither. "7 is prime" is a statement. "The sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean" is a statement, and a true one under simple random sampling. "Please pass the salt" is not a statement, it carries no truth value. "This sentence is false" is excluded from formal logic entirely, since assigning it either value produces a contradiction. Statements are usually written with lowercase letters like $p$, $q$, $r$.

1.2 Connectives

Statements combine through connectives, and each has a precise meaning that often differs from everyday English.

  • Negation $\neg p$ flips the truth value of $p$.
  • Conjunction $p \wedge q$ ("and") is true only when both are true.
  • Disjunction $p \vee q$ ("or") is true when at least one is true, including both. This is the inclusive or. Everyday English often means the exclusive version, "coffee or tea" usually implies not both, but logic's default $\vee$ allows both.
  • Implication $p \to q$ ("if $p$ then $q$") is false only when $p$ is true and $q$ is false. If $p$ is false, the implication is automatically true, regardless of $q$. This is the part that trips people up: "if it rains, I bring an umbrella" is not violated on a dry day whether or not I carry one. A false antecedent makes the whole conditional vacuously true, and that single fact underwrites several proof techniques below.
  • Biconditional $p \leftrightarrow q$ ("$p$ if and only if $q$") is true exactly when both sides match, both true or both false.

1.3 Necessary and sufficient conditions

"$p$ is sufficient for $q$" means $p$ guarantees $q$, written $p \to q$. "$p$ is necessary for $q$" means $q$ cannot happen without $p$, written $q \to p$, equivalently $\neg p \to \neg q$. A real-life example: passing the exam is necessary for graduating, since you cannot graduate without it, but it is not sufficient, since other requirements remain. In statistics, a large sample size is often necessary for a reliable estimate but not sufficient on its own; you also need randomness and low bias in how the sample was collected.

1.4 Converse, inverse, contrapositive

Given the implication $p \to q$, three related statements can be formed.

  • Converse: $q \to p$. Not logically equivalent to the original. "If it rains, the ground is wet" does not mean "if the ground is wet, it rained," since a hose could have wet it.
  • Inverse: $\neg p \to \neg q$. Also not equivalent.
  • Contrapositive: $\neg q \to \neg p$. This one is logically equivalent to $p \to q$, always. That equivalence is the backbone of proof by contrapositive, useful whenever proving the forward direction is awkward but reasoning from the negation of the conclusion is clean.

1.5 Quantifiers

The universal quantifier $\forall x$ ("for all $x$") asserts a property holds across an entire domain: $\forall x \in \mathbb{R},\ x^2 \geq 0$. One counterexample kills such a claim. The existential quantifier $\exists x$ ("there exists an $x$") asserts at least one instance exists: $\exists x \in \mathbb{N},\ x$ is even and prime, true because $x=2$ works. One example proves it.

Negating a quantified statement flips the quantifier and negates what follows: $\neg(\forall x,\ P(x))$ becomes $\exists x,\ \neg P(x)$, and $\neg(\exists x,\ P(x))$ becomes $\forall x,\ \neg P(x)$. "Not all students failed" becomes "at least one student passed," not "all students passed." Negating a universal claim gives an existential counterexample, not the opposite universal, and this is one of the most common errors when reading proofs.

1.6 Proof techniques

Direct proof. Assume $p$, chain logical steps forward, arrive at $q$.

Proof by contrapositive. Instead of proving $p \to q$ directly, prove $\neg q \to \neg p$, valid because of the equivalence noted above. Classic example: to prove "if $n^2$ is even, then $n$ is even," it is easier to prove "if $n$ is odd, then $n^2$ is odd." Write $n = 2k+1$, so $n^2 = 4k^2 + 4k + 1 = 2(2k^2+2k)+1$, which is odd by construction.

Proof by contradiction. Assume $p$ and $\neg q$ simultaneously, derive a logical impossibility, conclude the assumption was wrong. Euclid's proof that infinitely many primes exist works this way: assume only finitely many primes $p_1, \dots, p_n$ exist, form $N = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_n + 1$. $N$ is not divisible by any $p_i$ (each division leaves remainder 1), so either $N$ is prime itself or has a prime factor outside the list, contradicting the assumption that the list was complete.

Proof by induction. For claims about all natural numbers: prove the base case ($n=1$), then prove that if the claim holds for $n$, it holds for $n+1$. This is domino logic, each step knocks over the next indefinitely.

Counterexample. To disprove a universal claim, one example suffices, and this is usually the fastest tool available. Before attempting to prove a claim, it is often worth spending a moment hunting for a counterexample.

1.7 Axioms, theorems, lemmas, corollaries

An axiom is a starting assumption accepted without proof, such as "through any two points there is exactly one line." A theorem is a proven result of real significance. A lemma is a smaller proven result used as a stepping stone toward a larger theorem. A corollary is a result that follows almost immediately from a theorem, with little additional work.

In inference, a hypothesis test is essentially proof by contradiction dressed in probability. You assume the null hypothesis, which plays the role of $\neg q$, and if the observed data would be absurdly unlikely under that assumption, you reject it. This is not a direct proof that the alternative is true, it is the same contradiction structure, just probabilistic rather than certain. That asymmetry is exactly why you "fail to reject" a null hypothesis rather than "accept" it: failing to find a contradiction is not the same as proving the assumption correct, echoing the necessary and sufficient distinction from 1.3.

2. Sets

Where logic gives you a language for reasoning, sets give you the objects to reason about. Nearly every structure in statistics, from a sample space to a support, is a set.

2.1 What is a set

A set is an unordered collection of distinct objects, called its elements. Sets are usually named with capital letters, and $x \in A$ means $x$ is an element of $A$, while $x \notin A$ means it is not. A set can be described by listing its elements, $A = \{1, 2, 3\}$, or through set-builder notation, described next. Order and repetition do not matter: $\{1,2,3\} = \{3,2,1\} = \{1,1,2,3\}$, since duplicates collapse. Two sets are equal exactly when they contain the same elements.

2.2 Set-builder notation

Set-builder notation defines a set by a property rather than a listing: $A = \{x : P(x)\}$, read "the set of all $x$ such that $P(x)$ holds." For example, $\{x \in \mathbb{R} : x^2 < 4\} = (-2, 2)$, the open interval. This notation is where logic and sets meet directly, $P(x)$ is a statement in the sense of Section 1, and membership in $A$ is just that statement evaluating to true.

2.3 Special sets

A handful of sets recur constantly: the empty set $\emptyset$ (contains nothing), the naturals $\mathbb{N}$, the integers $\mathbb{Z}$, the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$, the reals $\mathbb{R}$, and the complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$. Each is nested inside the next: $\mathbb{N} \subseteq \mathbb{Z} \subseteq \mathbb{Q} \subseteq \mathbb{R} \subseteq \mathbb{C}$.

2.4 Subsets and power sets

$A \subseteq B$ means every element of $A$ is also in $B$; $A \subsetneq B$ additionally requires $A \neq B$, a proper subset. A subtle but important fact: $\emptyset \subseteq B$ for every set $B$, since the defining condition "for every $x \in \emptyset$, $x \in B$" is an implication with a false antecedent (there is no such $x$), making it vacuously true, a direct callback to 1.2. The power set $\mathcal{P}(A)$ is the set of all subsets of $A$, and if $|A| = n$ then $|\mathcal{P}(A)| = 2^n$. For $A = \{1, 2\}$, $\mathcal{P}(A) = \{\emptyset, \{1\}, \{2\}, \{1,2\}\}$, four subsets.

2.5 Set operations and De Morgan's laws

Given sets $A$ and $B$ inside a universal set $U$:

  • Union $A \cup B = \{x : x \in A \text{ or } x \in B\}$.
  • Intersection $A \cap B = \{x : x \in A \text{ and } x \in B\}$.
  • Difference $A \setminus B = \{x : x \in A \text{ and } x \notin B\}$.
  • Complement $A^c = \{x \in U : x \notin A\}$.

These map exactly onto the connectives from 1.2, union to $\vee$, intersection to $\wedge$, complement to $\neg$. That correspondence produces De Morgan's laws for sets:

$$(A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c \qquad (A \cap B)^c = A^c \cup B^c$$

which are the direct translation of $\neg(p \vee q) \equiv \neg p \wedge \neg q$ and $\neg(p \wedge q) \equiv \neg p \vee \neg q$.

2.6 Cartesian products

The Cartesian product $A \times B = \{(a, b) : a \in A,\ b \in B\}$ is the set of all ordered pairs drawn from $A$ and $B$. Rolling two dice has sample space $\{1,\dots,6\} \times \{1,\dots,6\}$, 36 equally likely outcomes. Cartesian products are the raw material out of which relations and functions, Section 3, are built.

2.7 Cardinality

$|A|$ denotes the size of a set. Finite sets have a definite count. Infinite sets split further: $\mathbb{N}$, $\mathbb{Z}$, and $\mathbb{Q}$ are countably infinite, meaning their elements can in principle be listed $1, 2, 3, \dots$ without missing any. $\mathbb{R}$ is uncountable, a strictly larger kind of infinity, provable through Cantor's diagonal argument. This distinction is exactly why discrete random variables (countable support) use probability mass functions while continuous random variables (uncountable support, typically an interval of $\mathbb{R}$) require probability density functions instead.

2.8 Sets in probability

A sample space $\Omega$ is a set of all possible outcomes, and an event is a subset of $\Omega$. "$A$ or $B$ occurs" is the event $A \cup B$; "$A$ and $B$ both occur" is $A \cap B$; "$A$ does not occur" is $A^c$. Two events are mutually exclusive when $A \cap B = \emptyset$. Every rule from 2.5, including De Morgan's laws, applies directly to computing probabilities of compound events.

3. Functions

A function is a controlled way of getting from one set to another. Once sets are in place, functions describe how they relate.

3.1 Relations and functions

A relation from $A$ to $B$ is any subset of $A \times B$, any collection of pairings, no restrictions. A function $f: A \to B$ is a special relation obeying two rules: every $a \in A$ must be paired with something (totality), and each $a$ can be paired with only one element of $B$ (well-definedness). "Is a square root of," from non-negative reals to reals, is a relation but not a function, since 4 pairs with both 2 and $-2$. $f(x) = x^2$ is a function, since every input produces exactly one output.

3.2 Domain, codomain, range

The domain is the set a function is defined on, $A$. The codomain is the declared target set, $B$. The range (or image) is the actual set of outputs produced, $\{f(a) : a \in A\} \subseteq B$, and it can be a proper subset of the codomain. For $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, $f(x) = x^2$, the codomain is all of $\mathbb{R}$, but the range is only $[0, \infty)$.

3.3 Injective, surjective, bijective

Injective (one-to-one): distinct inputs give distinct outputs, formally $f(a_1) = f(a_2) \Rightarrow a_1 = a_2$. $f(x) = 2x + 1$ on $\mathbb{R}$ is injective. $f(x) = x^2$ on $\mathbb{R}$ is not, since $f(-2) = f(2)$.

Surjective (onto): every element of the codomain is actually hit, meaning range equals codomain. $f(x) = x^3 : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is surjective. $f(x) = x^2 : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is not, since negative numbers are never reached.

Bijective: both injective and surjective at once, a perfect one-to-one correspondence between $A$ and $B$. Bijections are the formal tool for comparing the size of infinite sets, two sets have the same cardinality exactly when a bijection exists between them.

3.4 Composition

Given $f: A \to B$ and $g: B \to C$, the composite $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x))$ applies $f$ first, then $g$. Order matters, $g \circ f$ and $f \circ g$ are generally different, and the composite is only well-defined when the range of the inner function fits inside the domain of the outer one.

3.5 Inverse functions

A function $f: A \to B$ has an inverse $f^{-1}: B \to A$ exactly when $f$ is bijective, satisfying $f^{-1}(f(x)) = x$ for all $x \in A$ and $f(f^{-1}(y)) = y$ for all $y \in B$. Without both injectivity and surjectivity, no consistent inverse can be defined.

3.6 Functions in statistics

This is the payoff. A random variable is, formally, nothing more than a function $X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ mapping outcomes in a sample space to real numbers. Once that clicks, several ideas from your coursework fall into place at once: the support of $X$ is just its range, "$X$ is injective" is exactly the condition needed for a clean change-of-variables in transformation problems, and computing $P(X \in S)$ for some set $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is really asking for the size, under the underlying probability measure, of the preimage $X^{-1}(S) \subseteq \Omega$. An estimator, such as the sample mean $\bar{X}$, is likewise a function of the data, mapping a sample to a single real number, an estimate. Every property you prove about an estimator, unbiasedness, consistency, efficiency, is a statement about a function.
